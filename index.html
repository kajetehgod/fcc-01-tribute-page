Charles Messier

Charles Messier was a French astronomer who created a Catalog of Nebulae and Star Clusters (Catalog des Nébuleuses et des Amas d'Étoiles), one of the most famous lists of astronomical objects.

Messier was searching for comets, so the initial purpose of the catalog was to distinguish between comets and nebulae. Now Messier catalog is the set of 110 deep-sky objects, many of which are still referenced by their Messier numbers. Some well-known examples of the objects are M1 (Crab Nebula), M31 (Andromeda Galaxy), and M87 (Virgo A).

Important Facts

  • 1.

    Charles Messier was born in 1730 in Badonviller, France. He was the tenth of twelve children of Françoise Grandblaise and Nicolas Messier.

  • 2.

    The appearance of the great six-tailed comet in 1744 and the annular solar eclipse, which was visible from his hometown in 1748, influenced his interest in astronomy.

  • 3.

    In 1751 Messier started his career working for Joseph Nicolas Delisle, the astronomer of the French Navy, keeping records of his observations.

  • 4.

    In 1753 Messier first documented an observation of the Mercury transit. He then continued to work at Hôtel de Cluny (now the Musée national du Moyen Âge), and at the French Navy observatories.

  • 5.

    In 1764 Messier was made a fellow of the Royal Society.

  • 6.

    In 1769 he was elected a foreign member of the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences.

  • 7.

    In 1770 he was elected to the French Academy of Sciences.

  • 8.

    Messier published the first version of his catalog in 1774 in the journal of the French Academy of Sciences in Paris. At that time the catalog contained only 45 unnumbered objects.

  • 9.

    By 1780 the catalog had increased to 70 objects.

  • 10.

    The final version of the Messier catalog was published in 1781 in the Connaissance des Temps, an official yearly publication of astronomical objects in France, and contained 103 objects.

  • 11.

    During his life Messier discovered 13 comets and co-discovered one comet together with several other observers.

  • 12.

    Messier died in 1817 and was buried in Père Lachaise Cemetery in Paris. His grave is near the graves of Frédéric Chopin and Abraham-Louis Breguet.

  • 13.

    Messier objects M104 through M110 were added to the catalog between 1921 and 1967. In 1921 Nicolas Camille Flammarion added Messier 104. In 1947 Helen Sawyer Hogg added M105, M105, and M107. In 1960 Owen Gingerich added M108 and M109. In 1967 Kenneth Glyn Jones added M110.

  • 14.

    Modern amateur astronomers use Messier objects for visual study and astrophotography. They regularly organize Messier marathons, events dedicated to finding as many Messier objects as possible during one night.
